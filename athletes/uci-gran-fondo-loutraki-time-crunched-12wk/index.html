2 · Training Zones

The Point of Zones

Training zones aren't arbitrary numbers. Each zone targets a specific physiological system. Training in the wrong zone doesn't just reduce effectiveness — it actively harms your progression by creating the wrong type of fatigue without the right type of adaptation.

Your plan controls how much time you spend in each zone — the zones are the vocabulary, the plan is the sentence. Your only job is to hit the prescribed zone when a workout calls for it. One caution that holds for every methodology: Zone 3 is the "gray zone" — too hard to recover from, too easy to create real adaptation. Ride it only when prescribed.

BEFORE YOU START: FTP TEST REQUIRED

Complete an FTP test before the structured intervals begin. Without your FTP, every zone target in this plan is a percentage of an unknown number. Until you test, use RPE (Rate of Perceived Exertion) to guide intensity.

After testing, recalculate all zones: Zone watts = FTP × zone percentage. If you use Zwift, TrainerRoad, or a Garmin — update your FTP setting immediately after testing.

FTP Testing Protocol

When an FTP test appears on your calendar, here's how to execute the 20-minute protocol. There is one maximal effort — the 20-minute test. Everything before it is preparation.

  1. 12 minutes progressive warmup (Z1 building to Z2)
  2. 3 minutes at RPE 7 (moderate-hard), then 1 minute easy — just opening the legs
  3. 5-minute hard opener — ride this hard (RPE 8-9), NOT all-out. Its only job is to clear out your anaerobic system so the 20-minute number reflects sustainable power, not a sprint. This is not the test.
  4. 10 minutes easy recovery (Z1) — fully recover before the test
  5. 20-MINUTE TIME TRIAL — this is the test. Start at an effort you're confident you can hold, then build. Even pacing beats going out hard and fading. This sets your FTP.
  6. 10 minutes easy cooldown

Formula: FTP = 20-minute average power × 0.95

WARNING

Don't test when tired. Do FTP tests when fresh — not after a hard training week. The test result sets ALL your training zones for the next 6 weeks. An inaccurate test means 6 weeks of wrong-zone training.

Critical Notes on Using Zones

  • Easy means EASY. Zone 2 should feel almost too easy. If you're questioning whether you're going hard enough, you're in the right zone.
  • Hard means HARD. Zone 4+ intervals should leave you breathless. If you can hold a conversation, you're not in the right zone.
  • Don't fill the gap. The biggest mistake is spending too much time in Zone 3 (tempo). It feels productive but creates maximum fatigue with suboptimal adaptation.
  • Power > HR > RPE. Use power meter if available, heart rate as backup, RPE as last resort. Heart rate lags — use RPE for short intervals.

3 · How Adaptation Works

Every workout in this plan exists for a reason. Understanding the adaptation model helps you make smart decisions when life disrupts the plan.

The Foundational Model

STEP 1: STRESS

You apply a training stimulus (the workout). This creates controlled damage — micro-tears in muscle, glycogen depletion, cardiovascular stress.

STEP 2: RECOVERY

Your body repairs the damage. This takes 24-72 hours depending on workout intensity. Sleep, nutrition, and stress levels all affect recovery speed.

STEP 3: SUPERCOMPENSATION

Your body doesn't just repair — it overbuilds. You come back slightly stronger than before. This is the entire point of training.

STEP 4: REPEAT

Apply the next stress at the peak of supercompensation. Too soon = overtraining. Too late = detraining. The plan's timing handles this for you.

Where It Goes Wrong

THE #1 TRAINING MISTAKE

Going too hard on easy days and too easy on hard days. This puts you in the "gray zone" — maximum fatigue, minimum adaptation. Trust the zones. Easy days should feel embarrassingly easy. Hard days should be genuinely hard.

THE #2 TRAINING MISTAKE

Skipping recovery. Adaptation happens during rest, not during workouts. The workout is the stimulus. Sleep is where you get faster. Cutting sleep to fit in more training is counterproductive — you're removing the adaptation window.

Practical Rules

  • Never add intensity to an easy day. If the plan says Z2, stay in Z2. Even if you feel great.
  • Never add volume to a hard day. Hard days are about quality, not quantity. If you can do more, you didn't go hard enough.
  • If you miss a workout, skip it. Don't try to make it up by doubling the next day. The plan accounts for progressive overload — doubling creates injury risk.
  • If you're sick, stop. Training while sick extends illness duration by 2-3x. Take the days off. You'll lose less fitness from rest than from a week of half-effort sick training.
  • Sleep > training. If you have to choose between a 5am workout and 8 hours of sleep, choose sleep every time.

4 · Weekly Structure

Every week of your plan is built from the same four ingredients: a long ride, interval sessions, easy rides, and strength work. Your plan places them on specific days — follow the calendar. What matters here is understanding what each session type is for, because riders who understand the purpose execute better than riders who just follow orders.

Your week has 5 training days, 3 of which are key sessions. Key sessions are the ones that drive adaptation. Everything else supports them.

Session Types Explained

LONG RIDE

The backbone of endurance preparation. Primarily Zone 2 with race-specific efforts mixed in during Build and Peak phases. Build duration progressively — don't jump to your peak duration of 2.1-3.5 hours in Week 1.

INTERVALS

High-intensity sessions targeting specific energy systems. Follow the prescribed zones exactly. Full recovery between intervals is mandatory — cutting rest reduces the training stimulus.

EASY RIDE

Zone 1-2 only. Active recovery. The purpose is blood flow, not fitness. If you catch yourself hammering a Strava segment on an easy day, you're doing it wrong.

STRENGTH

Cycling-specific strength work. Focus on single-leg exercises, core stability, and hip strength. Keep it under 60 minutes. Heavy enough to be challenging, light enough to not wreck your legs for the next bike session.

When Life Interferes

The plan owns the schedule. When real life collides with it, these principles keep the damage contained:

  • Protect the key sessions first. If a week falls apart, keep one interval session and the long ride; let everything else go easy or disappear.
  • Never stack key sessions back-to-back to "catch up" — that creates fatigue without proportional adaptation. A missed session stays missed.
  • Illness: drop ALL intensity. Easy riding only if you feel up to it. Symptoms below the neck (chest, body aches) mean complete rest.

5 · Phase Progression

Your 12-week plan moves through 4 phases, each with a specific purpose. The exact week ranges are in your training calendar — what matters here is why each phase exists, so you don't rush base to get to the "hard stuff." Base fitness determines your ceiling.

Base

The foundation. Mostly easy aerobic riding with progressive volume, plus strength work. This phase feels too easy — that's the point. Aerobic base determines your ceiling; there is no shortcut around it. Long rides build progressively. Your weekly budget limits ride length, so each long ride counts double — use them for race-pace fueling practice.

Build

Race-specific intensity enters: threshold and VO2max intervals, long rides with race-pace efforts. This is where it gets hard. Fatigue is expected — it's being managed deliberately through the recovery weeks your plan schedules.

Peak

Maximum specificity. Race-simulation rides, dress-rehearsal fueling, intensity stays high while volume starts to drop. You should feel fitness arriving.

Taper

Volume drops sharply; short, sharp efforts keep the engine awake. You cannot gain fitness now — you can only show up fresh or show up tired. Trust the training and rest.

RECOVERY WEEKS

Your plan schedules regular recovery weeks — volume drops 30-40%, intensity drops to easy riding only. These weeks are where adaptation actually happens. Skipping them is the fastest path to overtraining and stalled progress. They are not optional.

6 · Workout Execution

UNTIL YOU COMPLETE YOUR FTP TEST

Your ZWO files use FTP-based power targets. Without your FTP number, use RPE (Rate of Perceived Exertion) from the zone chart instead. After your Week 1 FTP test, update your FTP in Zwift/TrainerRoad/Garmin and all workout targets will calibrate automatically.

The Execution Gap

The plan says: "4x4min at 110% FTP with 4min recovery."

What actually happens:

  • First interval: nailed it, felt strong
  • Second interval: still good, maybe went a bit hard
  • Third interval: struggling, cut 30 seconds short
  • Fourth interval: abandoned or at 95% FTP instead of 110%

Result: You did a Zone 3-4 workout instead of a Zone 5 workout. Different stimulus, different adaptation, wrong training effect.

Universal Execution Rules

RULE 1: START CONSERVATIVE

First interval of every set should feel slightly too easy. If the plan says 110% FTP, start the first rep at 105%. Build into it. The goal is completing ALL intervals at target power, not crushing the first one and failing the rest.

RULE 2: QUALITY OVER QUANTITY

If you can't hold target power, STOP the workout. 3 good intervals at 110% FTP are worth more than 5 intervals where the last two are at 95%. Bad reps don't count — they just create fatigue.

RULE 3: RECOVERY MEANS RECOVERY

Between intervals, drop to Zone 1. Not Zone 2, not "easy Zone 3." Zone 1. Full recovery ensures the next interval hits the right energy system.

RULE 4: CADENCE MATTERS

Alternate between high cadence (95-105 rpm) and low cadence (60-75 rpm) intervals. High cadence trains the cardiovascular system; low cadence builds muscular force. A complete rider develops both — the range to spin smoothly when fresh and to push a big gear when the road tilts up.

Indoor vs Outdoor Workouts

Best done indoors: Short intervals (VO2max, anaerobic), FTP tests, cadence drills, recovery spins.

Best done outdoors: Long rides, tempo efforts, race simulations, skills practice, group rides.

Balance: Aim for at least 1-2 outdoor rides per week. Indoor training is efficient but doesn't prepare you for real-world race conditions — wind, terrain changes, group dynamics, and the mental challenge of a long day in the saddle.

When and How to Modify Workouts

  • Feeling great: Don't add volume or intensity. Stick to the plan. Save the good legs for the next hard day.
  • Feeling tired: Drop intensity by 5% but keep duration. If that's still too much, convert to Zone 2.
  • Feeling terrible: Convert to easy spin or take the day off. One missed workout doesn't matter. Two weeks of subpar training does.
  • Missed workout rule: Never try to make up a missed workout. Just move forward in the plan.

7 · Recovery Protocol

Adaptation happens during recovery, not during training. The workout is the stimulus. Recovery is where you actually get faster.

Daily Recovery Protocol

IMMEDIATELY POST-RIDE
  • 30g protein + 60-90g carbs within 30 minutes
  • Rehydrate: 150% of fluid lost (weigh before/after)
  • Compression garments for rides > 3 hours
EVENING
  • 8+ hours sleep (non-negotiable)
  • No screens 60 min before bed
  • Cool room (65-68°F / 18-20°C)
  • Light stretching or foam rolling (10 min max)
AFTER HARD SESSIONS
  • 10-minute cooldown spin (mandatory)
  • Extra 200-300 calories in recovery meal
  • No hard training for 48 hours
  • Next day should be easy or rest
RECOVERY WEEKS
  • Volume drops 30-40%
  • No Zone 4+ intensity
  • Extra sleep if possible
  • This is where fitness consolidates

HRV: What It Is and How to Use It

Heart Rate Variability (HRV) measures the time between heartbeats. Higher variability = better recovery. Lower variability = accumulated stress.

  • Good for: Tracking long-term recovery trends (7-day rolling average), identifying overtraining before it happens
  • Not good for: Making single-day training decisions, replacing how you feel
  • How to use: Measure every morning before getting out of bed. If your 7-day average drops more than 10% below baseline, take an extra recovery day.
  • Apps: HRV4Training, Elite HRV, Whoop

Signs You Need More Recovery

  • Resting heart rate elevated 5+ bpm above normal
  • Can't hit power targets despite feeling "fine"
  • Motivation drops for more than 3 consecutive days
  • Sleep quality declining despite good sleep hygiene
  • Persistent muscle soreness beyond 48 hours
  • Getting sick more than usual

9 · Nutrition Strategy

You can have perfect training, a dialed bike, and excellent pacing strategy. None of it matters if you run out of fuel halfway through your race.

Fueling errors are the most common preventable cause of bad race days — the difference between finishing strong and crawling to the line.

The Reality Check

The cycling nutrition industry wants you to believe you need seventeen different products, each with proprietary blend ratios and specific timing windows measured in seconds. You don't.

You need carbohydrates during exercise. You need protein and carbs after exercise. You need reasonable daily nutrition that supports training. That's 95% of it. Everything else is optimization you can worry about after you've nailed the basics.

Daily Nutrition for Training

Your body is either recovering from the last workout or preparing for the next one. Daily nutrition supports both.

Timing That Actually Matters

PRE-WORKOUT (2-3 HOURS BEFORE)

If training hard (threshold, VO2max):

  • 1-2g carbs per kg bodyweight
  • Low fiber, low fat, moderate protein
  • Examples: oatmeal with banana and honey, or toast with peanut butter

If training easy (Z2 endurance):

  • Eat normally, don't stress timing
  • Can even train fasted if under 90 minutes

The rule: Hard sessions need fuel. Easy sessions are flexible.

POST-WORKOUT (0-90 MINUTES AFTER)

If workout was long (2.5+ hours) AND hard, AND you have another hard session within 24-36 hours:

  • 20-30g protein
  • 1-1.5g carbs per kg bodyweight
  • Liquid is fine (protein shake, chocolate milk)

If workout was easy, short, or your next hard session is 48+ hours away:

  • Just eat your next meal normally
  • Recovery nutrition is optional

The rule: The more frequently you train hard, the more critical recovery nutrition becomes.

Supplements

Most supplements are placebo at best, actively harmful at worst.

WORTH TAKING
  • Vitamin D (2000-4000 IU daily): Supports bone health, immune function, recovery. Most people are deficient. Get levels tested.
  • Creatine monohydrate (5g daily): Improves high-intensity repeatability. Useful for VO2max and sprint work. Cheap, well-researched, safe.
  • Caffeine (3-6mg/kg before hard sessions): Proven performance enhancer. Coffee works, pills work, gels work. Tolerance builds — cycle off occasionally.
NOT WORTH TAKING
  • BCAAs: Complete waste if you eat adequate protein
  • Testosterone boosters: Scams
  • Fat burners: Scams with side effects
  • Recovery drinks with proprietary blends: Overpriced protein + carbs

The rule: If you're deficient in something (Vitamin D, iron), fix it. If you're considering a supplement to “optimize,” ask yourself if you've already nailed sleep, nutrition basics, and training consistency. If not, fix those first.

Fueling During Workouts

This is where races are won or lost.

For any ride over 90 minutes at moderate-to-high intensity (Z3+), you need 60-80g of carbohydrates per hour. Your gut can absorb approximately 60g of glucose per hour through SGLT1 transporters. Add fructose (which uses different transporters) and you can push to 90g total. The target for most athletes is 70-75g per hour — enough to fuel hard efforts without GI distress.

Duration-Scaled Race Fueling

This is the key insight most nutrition advice gets wrong: carb intake should scale DOWN with race duration, not up. At lower intensities (longer races), your body shifts toward fat oxidation. Forcing 90g/hr of carbs into a system running at 44% VO2max exceeds physiological absorption capacity and causes GI distress.

Hydration

You lose 0.5-2 liters of fluid per hour depending on temperature, humidity, and effort. Dehydration of 2-3% bodyweight impairs performance.

  • Aim for: 500-750ml per hour (16-25 oz). More in heat, less in cold.
  • Short rides (<90 min): Water is fine
  • Long rides (2+ hours): Electrolyte drink with sodium (500-1000mg sodium/hour through sweat)
  • Don't overthink potassium, magnesium, or trace minerals. Sodium is 90% of what matters for performance.
CRAMPING

Cramps are NOT caused by electrolyte deficiency (despite what the supplement industry tells you). Cramps are caused by neuromuscular fatigue — your muscles are tired and misfiring. Salt tabs might help by changing neuromuscular excitability, but the real fix is better pacing and better training.

Training Your Gut

Your gut is trainable just like your muscles. If you never eat during training rides, your gut won't tolerate eating during races. SGLT1 transporters double in ~2 weeks of training (GSSI), but full adaptation takes 6-12 weeks.

  • Base phase: Practice eating real food on easy rides. 40-50g carbs/hour, mostly solid. Build tolerance gradually.
  • Build phase: Increase to 60-70g carbs/hour. Mix liquids and solids. Practice eating at tempo pace.
  • Peak phase: Hit your race-day target. Race nutrition only (gels, drink, bars you'll use on race day). Practice at race pace.
  • Race week: Stick with what worked in training. No new products. Trust your gut (literally).

Race-Day Nutrition Execution

Everything you practiced in training gets executed under stress.

PRE-RACE MEAL (3-4 HOURS BEFORE START)

Goal: Top off glycogen. Don't experiment.

  • 2-3g carbs per kg bodyweight
  • Moderate protein, low fat, low fiber
  • Familiar foods only
  • Examples: oatmeal with banana and honey, toast with jam and peanut butter, rice with eggs
  • Drink 500ml water with your meal
STARTING LINE (30-60 MIN BEFORE START)

Take one gel (24g carbs) with 200ml water. You're not adding meaningful glycogen — you're making sure blood glucose is stable as the race starts.

THE FUELING TIMELINE

The most common mistake: waiting until you're hungry to start eating. By the time you feel hungry, your glycogen is already depleted and your brain is glucose-starved. You're in a hole you can't climb out of.

WindowAction
0-30 minFocus on pacing and positioning. Sip on drink, don't force nutrition yet.
30-60 minFirst gel or equivalent (24g carbs). Start your fueling clock.
Every 30 min afterConsume carbs consistently. Gel every 30 min (48g/hr) + drink mix (20-30g/hr) = 70-80g total.
Aid stationsTop off bottles. Grab food if needed. Keep moving — you're not on a picnic.

Set a timer. Seriously. Your brain will be stupid. It will forget to eat. It will lie to you and say “I'm fine, I'll eat at the next aid station.” The timer removes decision-making.

WHEN YOUR STOMACH REBELS

It will. At some point in a long race, your gut will protest. High-intensity exercise diverts blood from your GI system to working muscles. Food sits there, undigested.

  1. Back off intensity for 5-10 minutes. Drop to Z2 pace. Let gut blood flow recover.
  2. Switch to liquid calories temporarily. Easier to digest than solids. Sports drink or cola at aid stations.
  3. Small sips, not big gulps. Easier on the stomach, less sloshing.
  4. Don't panic and stop eating entirely. You'll bonk 30 minutes later. Maintain some carb intake even if reduced.

What NOT to do: Hammer harder while nauseous. You'll either vomit or shut down completely.

Weight Management vs Performance

You want to be lean for racing. But chasing leanness during a training block is self-sabotage.

THE TRAINING BLOCK RULE

During your training build, your job is to train hard and recover well. Not cut weight. Energy deficit impairs recovery. Under-fueled training produces inferior workouts.

When to cut weight: After your race. During the off-season. When training volume is low and intensity is moderate. A 500-calorie daily deficit over 8-12 weeks can drop 4-6kg without destroying your fitness.

The rule: Chase performance metrics, not scale numbers. If your FTP and climbing times improve, your weight is fine. If they're declining, eat more.

THE RACE DAY FUELING RULE

On race day, eat what you've practiced. If you haven't tested a gel brand in training, don't eat it in the race. GI distress at mile 60 of a 69.6-mile race is a DNF-level problem that is 100% preventable.

10 · Mental Preparation

A 69.6-mile race is as much a mental challenge as a physical one. Your body will want to quit before it needs to. Your brain will lie to you about how bad it is. Having a mental strategy is not optional.

The 6-2-7 Breathing Technique

When things get hard (and they will), use this breathing pattern:

6s
Inhale
2s
Hold
7s
Exhale

Deep belly breath through the nose. Brief pause at the top. Slow, controlled exhale through the mouth. Longer exhale = parasympathetic activation.

When to use: Before the start, during long climbs, when you want to quit, at aid stations, during the dark mile.

Process Goals vs Outcome Goals

  • Bad goal: "Finish in under 8 hours." (You can't control weather, mechanicals, or how you feel.)
  • Good goal: "Fuel every 30 minutes. Stay in my zones. Ride my race." (100% within your control.)

On race day, focus exclusively on process goals. Check in every 30 minutes: Am I fueling? Am I hydrating? Am I in my zone? If yes, you're having a perfect race regardless of the clock.

Mantras That Work

Pick 2-3 short phrases and practice them in training. Say them out loud on hard efforts.

  • "This is what I trained for."
  • "One pedal stroke at a time."
  • "I've done harder things than this."
  • "Fuel, hydrate, breathe. Repeat."

The Quit Test

When you want to quit during the race (you will), ask yourself these three questions:

  1. Have I eaten in the last 30 minutes? If no, eat first. You might just be bonking.
  2. Have I been drinking? If no, drink 500ml. Dehydration mimics exhaustion.
  3. Am I actually injured or just uncomfortable? Discomfort is part of the race. Actual injury (sharp pain, swelling) is a reason to stop.

If you've eaten, hydrated, and aren't injured — keep going. The feeling will pass.

11 · Race Week

Race week is about arriving at the start line fresh, confident, and prepared. You cannot gain fitness in the last week. You CAN lose it by doing too much. Your plan has the taper schedule — follow it. The principles below are what the schedule is protecting.

Race Week Principles

  • No fitness tests. The urge to "check the legs" with one more hard effort spends race-day matches. Heavy legs mid-week are the taper working, not fitness leaving.
  • Nothing new. No new equipment, food, or routines. Race week is execution, not experimentation.
  • Front-load the logistics. Bike serviced, gear laid out, and bags packed early in the week — the night before is for sleeping, not wrenching.
  • Sleep is cumulative. Bank 8-9 hours per night all week. The night before the race is usually bad for everyone — it also matters least if the week was good.

Pre-Race Checklist

  • Bike serviced and in race-ready condition (do this by mid-week, not the night before)
  • Tire pressure set for conditions (road: follow your tire AND rim manufacturer's recommended PSI range — the lower of the two limits wins)
  • Nutrition packed and tested (same brands/products used in training)
  • Bottles filled with your tested hydration mix
  • Drop bags prepared (if applicable)
  • Race number attached to bike or person
  • Phone charged, emergency contacts accessible
  • Weather checked — have a Plan B for layers
  • Know aid station locations and what they provide
  • Alarm set with buffer time (be at the start 45 minutes early)
CARB LOADING DONE RIGHT

Carb loading starts 2-3 days before the race. Increase carb intake to 8-10g/kg body weight. This isn't "eat everything in sight" — it's strategically increasing the carb portion of normal meals. Rice, pasta, bread, potatoes, fruit. Reduce fiber and fat intake to avoid GI issues.

Race Execution Strategy

  • First 25% of race: Hold back. Ride 5% below target power/effort. Bank energy.
  • Middle 50% of race: Settle into target effort. Fuel relentlessly. Every 20-30 minutes.
  • Final 25% of race: If you feel good, increase effort slightly. If you're struggling, maintain. Do not blow up.
  • Fueling alarm: Set a recurring timer for every 20 minutes. Eat whether you feel like it or not.
THE GOLDEN RULE

Start slower than you think you should. Every experienced endurance racer will tell you the same thing: the race doesn't start until the last third. Everyone goes out too hard. Be the one who doesn't.

  • Eat a real meal within 60 minutes (protein + carbs + fat)
  • Light walking to keep blood flowing
  • No training for 3-5 days minimum
  • Celebrate. You just raced Gran Fondo Loutraki 69.6mi. That's not nothing.

13 · Road Skills

Road racing rewards bike-handling and positioning as much as fitness. A rider who can hold a wheel, corner at speed, and read a group will beat a stronger rider who burns matches fighting for position. These skills are trainable — practice them deliberately.

Riding in the Pack

HOLDING A WHEEL
  • Sit 30–60cm off the wheel in front — close enough to draft (20–30% energy saving), far enough to react.
  • Look through and past the rider ahead, not at their wheel. Your bike follows your eyes.
  • Match speed with the brakes feathered, not grabbed — surging and braking is how gaps and crashes start.
  • Never overlap your front wheel with someone's rear wheel. A small swerve takes you down.
POSITIONING & CROSSWINDS
  • Stay in the front third before climbs, corners, and crosswind sections — moving up costs energy, getting shelled costs the race.
  • In a crosswind the draft is diagonal: shelter sits behind and to the leeward side, forming echelons. Know which side the wind is from.
  • Move up on the sheltered side and at the front of surges, not by fighting through the gutter.

Cornering on Tarmac

THE FAST LINE
  • Brake in a straight line BEFORE the corner; be off the brakes by the apex.
  • Outside-in-outside: enter wide, clip the apex, drift out. Outside foot down and weighted.
  • Look at the exit, not the apex. In a group, hold your line — predictability keeps everyone upright.
  • In the wet, brake earlier and lean less; paint, manhole covers, and oil are ice.

Descending

SPEED WITH CONTROL
  • Hands in the drops — lower centre of gravity and full brake leverage.
  • Feather both brakes before corners; trail off as you tip in. Don't brake mid-corner.
  • Relax your upper body. A tense rider fights the bike; a loose rider lets it track.
  • On a known descent, scout the tight corners. On an unknown one, ride within sight lines.

Handling Drills

Ten minutes of drills per week builds race-day confidence.

  • Bottle grabs: reach for your bottle while holding a straight line. Both hands, eyes up.
  • Shoulder rubs: with a trusted rider, lean shoulder-to-shoulder on grass at low speed — contact stops being scary.
  • Cornering laps: a quiet bend, increasing entry speed each lap until you find the limit. No traffic, no heroics.
  • Paceline rotation: with a small group, practise smooth through-and-off so pulls are even and seamless.

14 · Heat Training

We don't have confirmed climate data for Gran Fondo Loutraki — and our coaching read is the block below is worth running either way: the aerobic carryover travels.

HEAT TRAINING: RECOMMENDED

If your race runs hot (check its race page), treat the block as required prep (see the hot-condition evidence above). If it runs cool, it's recommended, not required — the cool-condition evidence for a performance benefit is split, not settled, and we say so.

The Protocol

HEAT PROTOCOL (WEEKS 9-10 OF YOUR PLAN)

A concentrated 2-week block, ending close to race week so the adaptations don't fade before you need them — they decay at roughly 2.5% per day once you stop.

  • 5-6 sessions across the 2 weeks — roughly every other day, not daily.
  • Post-exercise passive heat: 15-20 min sauna, hot bath, or extra layers during/after a normal training session. Add duration gradually.
  • What adapts first: plasma volume expansion and earlier sweat onset show up in the first few sessions; sweat-rate and core-temperature adaptations mature by the end of the block.
HEAT SAFETY
  • Weigh yourself before and after — replace fluid losses, don't guess
  • Never stack a heat session on a hard training day or when you're sick
  • Stop immediately for dizziness, nausea, confusion, or goosebumps in the heat
  • Get medical clearance first if you're on cardiovascular medication or pregnant
